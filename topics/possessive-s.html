We use the apostrophe s ('s), also called possessive 's, as a determiner to show that something belongs to someone or something.

Basic Ownership

The apostrophe is placed on the owner, and the item follows.

Is that Olivia's bag?

Britain's coastline is very beautiful.

Complex Noun Phrases

The possessive 's can be used in complex noun phrases and can even appear twice in the same phrase.

Greg is her youngest daughter's husband.

We went to Jake's father's funeral.

Possessive 's Rules: Singular vs. Plural

The position of the apostrophe depends entirely on whether the noun is singular or plural.

Singular Noun

Use 's after a singular noun.

FORM: Noun + 's

Example: The girl's bedroom (one girl)

Regular Plural Noun

Use ' after a plural noun that ends in -s.

FORM: Plural Noun + 's

Example: The girls' bedroom (more than one girl)

Irregular Plural Noun

Use 's with irregular plural nouns (e.g., children, men).

FORM: Irregular Noun + 's

Example: The children's parents...

Example: other people's property.

Possessive 's for Time and Duration

We also use possessive 's to talk about time and duration.

Time (Periods)

Used for specific time units like days, weeks, or months.

Is that yesterday's paper?

Duration (Length of Time)

Used to express how long something lasts.

I've only had one week's holiday so far this year.
